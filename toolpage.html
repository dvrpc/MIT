Complete Streets

Context-sensitive bicycle and pedestrian accommodations should be pursued throughout the region as part of a Complete Streets policy framework. Complete Streets design standards make roads safer and more user-friendly for bicyclists, pedestrians, the disabled, the elderly, and drivers by including wide sidewalks, bicycle lanes, raised crosswalks and medians, audible traffic signals, bus pullouts, and any other design elements that support safe, alternative transportation. Complete Street design also includes sustainable measures to treat stormwater runoff and minimize or eliminate water accumulation at intersections or other points along the street. To make room for multiple modes, municipalities may pursue road diets: usually conversions of four-lane undivided roads into three lanes (two travel lanes and a continuous two-way left-turn lane). The remaining pavement width from the eliminated lane can be converted into bicycle lanes, sidewalks, on-street parking, or some combination of these elements.

NJDOT was among the first state DOTs to adopt an internal Complete Streets policy for all new and retrofit transportation facilities funded through their Capital Program. Camden and Mercer Counties have countywide policies and every municipality in Mercer County has a Complete Streets policy.
